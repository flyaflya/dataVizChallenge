Question 1: Hypothesis Formation - Why might high altitude affect baseball performance? Is Coors Field affected by high altitude?

Answer: Why high altitude affects baseball performance High altitude changes the physics of how the ball moves through the air and also affects players. The main mechanisms are:

Primary Mechanisms of High Altitude Effects:

  1. Reduced air density ‚Üí less aerodynamic drag Batted balls lose less speed, so fly farther on average. This increases the probability a long fly becomes an extra-base hit or a home run.
  2. Reduced Magnus effect ‚Üí less pitch movement Breaking pitches (curveballs, sliders) rely on airflow to generate movement. Thinner air reduces that movement, making breaking pitches flatter and easier to hit.
  3. Temperature and humidity interactions Higher temperature and lower humidity (often correlated with altitude and local climate) can further reduce drag and make the ball ‚Äúlivelier.‚Äù
  4. Human/strategic effects Player endurance and recovery can be impacted at altitude; teams sometimes change roster/strategy (pitching usage, ball handling) to adapt. Because these physical effects directly change batted-ball distance and pitch movement, stadiums at high elevation tend to show higher run-scoring and home-run rates compared with sea-level parks (all else equal).

Is Coors Field affected by high altitude? Yes. Coors Field in Denver sits at about 5,280 feet above sea level ‚Äî the highest elevation of any Major League ballpark. That elevation is large enough to produce noticeable effects:

  • Historically, Coors Field has had higher rates of runs and home runs per game than most other MLB parks, consistent with reduced drag and reduced pitch movement.
  • The Rockies introduced a humidor for baseballs (implemented in 2002) to store balls at controlled humidity. The humidor reduces ball liveliness and mitigates, but does not completely eliminate, the altitude effect.
  • Other non-altitude factors (park dimensions, wind, team composition, rule changes, league-wide offense trends) also influence run rates, so altitude is an important but not sole contributor.

How to test this with your dataset (quick suggestions) - Compare Coors Field (‚ÄúCOL‚Äù) vs league: compute average runs/game and HR/game and show percent above league average. - Visual checks: horizontal bar charts (or two-facet 2010 vs 2021) that highlight ‚ÄúCOL‚Äù. - Statistical checks: simple t-test or bootstrap confidence intervals for runs/HR per game between COL and other parks; compare pre-/post-humidor if you have earlier seasons.

Question 1: Data Aggregation Strategy - How many games are in the dataset? Why do we aggregate individual games into stadium averages before we start the visualization process?

Answer: There are 2,430 games in the 2010 file and 2,429 games in the 2021 file (4,859 games total). We aggregate individual games into stadium averages for several practical and statistical reasons:

  • Statistical stability: single games are noisy (pitcher matchups, weather, randomness). Averaging across all home games at a stadium reduces variance and reveals the persistent stadium-level effect.
  • Signal vs noise: aggregation amplifies systematic park effects (altitude, dimensions, humidor) and de-emphasizes transient game-to-game noise.
  • Appropriate unit of inference: the business question is about the stadium (‚ÄúIs Coors Field run-friendly?‚Äù), so stadium-level averages match the decision unit.
  • Readability & communication: ~30 stadium points are easier to read and compare (rankings, percent-above-league) than thousands of game-level points.
  • Visualization design: aggregates prevent overplotting and let you use accurate encodings (bar lengths, ordered lists, annotations) that support precise comparisons.

Below is a small, ready-to-paste pandas snippet that computes per-stadium game counts and averages for runs and home runs (useful before plotting):

# Compute per-stadium averages and game counts
agg_2010 = (df2010
    .assign(totalRuns = df2010.homeScore + df2010.visScore,

Importantü§î Discussion Questions: Stage 3 - Structuring of Visual Mappings

Question 1: Geometry Choices - Why is a horizontal bar chart better than a scatter plot for this data? - When would you choose a vertical bar chart over horizontal?

Answer: Horizontal vs. scatter / vertical (concise answer):

  • Horizontal bar charts are better here because stadiums are categorical and bar length encodes the numeric value (average runs) using position/length ‚Äî a highly accurate perceptual channel. Scatter plots imply two continuous variables and are less clear when the x-axis is just category labels; they also make reading and comparing many category labels awkward.
  • Use a vertical bar chart when you have few categories, short labels, or when the x-axis encodes an ordered or temporal variable (time series). Horizontal bars are preferable for many categories, long labels, or ranking tasks because labels are easier to read and comparisons remain precise.

Question 2: Aesthetic Mappings - What does the color highlighting accomplish in Approach 4? - How does position (x/y) compare to color for encoding data?

Answer: Color highlighting (Approach 4)

  • Creates a clear visual hierarchy: the highlighted stadium (COL) immediately becomes the focal point while other stadiums recede to secondary importance.
  • Improves pattern detection: color helps viewers find the stadium of interest quickly without scanning labels or bar lengths.
  • Supports storytelling: it emphasizes the specific claim you‚Äôre testing (e.g., ‚ÄúIs Coors Field unusually run-friendly?‚Äù) and makes the intended comparison explicit.
  • Works as a visual filter: color can be used to group or flag categories (highlight, above/below average, region), aiding viewers who skim the chart.

Position vs. Color for encoding data

  • Position (x/y) is the most accurate perceptual channel for quantitative values. Use position (bar length or axis position) for the primary numeric variable (average runs) when precise comparison is required.
  • Color is best used as a secondary, categorical or emphasis channel. It quickly draws attention and encodes group membership but is less precise for reading exact numeric differences.
  • Combine them: encode the numeric value with position and use color to add emphasis or category information (e.g., highlight COL in purple while encoding runs with bar length). This preserves accuracy while supporting narrative emphasis.
  • Accessibility note: when relying on color, ensure sufficient contrast and provide redundant encodings (labels, annotations, or shape) for colorblind or grayscale viewers.

What Color Highlighting Accomplishes in Approach 4:

  1. Visual Hierarchy:
    • Dark purple highlights Colorado (COL) as the primary focus
    • Light grey makes other stadiums fade into the background
    • Creates a clear ‚Äúhero‚Äù element that draws immediate attention
  2. Pattern Recognition:
    • Color acts as a visual filter, helping viewers quickly locate the stadium of interest
    • Reduces cognitive load by pre-selecting the most important data point
    • Makes the chart‚Äôs message immediately clear: ‚ÄúColorado is the standout‚Äù
  3. Business Communication:
    • Reinforces the hypothesis being tested (Is Colorado the most run-friendly?)
    • Provides visual proof of the claim in the chart title
    • Makes the chart presentation-ready for stakeholders

Position vs. Color for Encoding Data:

Position (x/y coordinates): - Strengths: - Most accurate for quantitative comparisons - Allows precise value estimation - Works well for continuous data - Excellent for showing relationships and trends

  • Limitations:
    • Requires more cognitive effort to interpret
    • Less effective for highlighting specific categories
    • Can be cluttered with many data points

Color: - Strengths: - Excellent for categorical distinctions - Creates immediate visual impact - Great for highlighting and emphasis - Effective for grouping and filtering

  • Limitations:
    • Less precise for quantitative comparisons
    • Can be problematic for colorblind users
    • Limited number of distinguishable colors
    • Cultural associations may vary

Best Practice: Use position for the primary data encoding (bar length for runs) and color for secondary information (highlighting Colorado). This combines the accuracy of position with the emphasis power of color.

colorado_index = avgDF_2010_sorted[avgDF_2010_sorted.home == "COL"].index[0]
